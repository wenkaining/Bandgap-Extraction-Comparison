Introduction The reliability and structural integrity of materials are generally governed by their interaction with the environment and the multi-physics phenomena resulting from those interactions.
Stress corrosion cracking (SCC) is one of these phenomena, arguably the most common yet complex failure mechanism of engineering structures and components (Turnbull, 2001).
SCC involves the propagation of cracks due to the combined action of mechanical stresses and the environment.
It is often facilitated by pre-existing defects, such as cor- 2rosion pits, and takes place in a broad range of metallic materials and envi- ronments (Raja and Shoji, 2011).
However, and despite decades of intensive research, the underlying physical mechanisms governing SCC are still not well understood (Turnbull, 1993; Crane and GangloÔ¨Ä, 2016).
A number of mechanistic interpretations have been presented, and models can be classiÔ¨Åed into two categories.
The Ô¨Årst one refers to models and mechanisms where crack propagation is driven by the anodic corrosion reaction at the crack tip, including the active path dissolution model (Parkins, 1996) or models grounded on the Ô¨Ålm rupture-dissolution-repassivation (FRDR) mechanism (Scully, 1975, 1980; Andresen and Ford, 1988).
The second category in- cludes models where crack growth is cathodic-driven and associated with the ingress and transport of hydrogen, such as the hydrogen-enhanced decohe- sion (HEDE) mechanism (Oriani, 1972; Serebrinsky et al., 2004; Kristensen et al., 2020), hydrogen enhanced localized plasticity (HELP) (Sofronis and Birnbaum, 1995) or adsorption-induced dislocation emission (AIDE) (Lynch, 1988).
The characteristics of SCC can vary signiÔ¨Åcantly from one material to another, or even between diÔ¨Äerent environments within the same material, hindering a critical evaluation of the diÔ¨Äerent mechanistic interpretations.
Moreover, many of these mechanisms are not mutually exclusive and could be acting in concert.
The development of predictive models for SCC is not only hindered by the complexity of the physical picture but also by the challenges associ- ated with encapsulating into a numerical framework the diÔ¨Äerent physics of the problem (diÔ¨Äusion, electrochemistry, mechanics).
It is particularly 3challenging from a mathematical and computational perspective to capture how the metal-electrolyte interface evolves over time.
SCC failures typically involve the growth of multiple pits, a pit-to-crack transition stage and the propagation of one or multiple cracks (Larrosa et al., 2018).
Capturing the complex morphologies resulting from this three-stage process is not an easy task and typically requires deÔ¨Åning moving interfacial boundary conditions and manually adjusting the interface topology with arbitrary criteria when merging or division occurs.
Numerical methods have been developed to cap- ture the moving boundary problem of localised corrosion; these include the eXtended Finite Element Method (X-FEM) (Duddu et al., 2016), Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) techniques (Sun et al., 2014) and peridynamics (Chen and Bobaru, 2015).
However, progress is often limited by the inability to deal with arbitrary 3D geometries, cumbersome implementations or the computational cost.
An alternative approach is the use of the phase Ô¨Åeld method, which is revolutionising the modelling of moving boundaries and interfacial problems.
In the phase Ô¨Åeld paradigm, the boundary conditions at an interface are replaced by a diÔ¨Äerential equation for the evolution of an auxiliary (phase) Ô¨Åeld œÜ.
This phase Ô¨Åeld takes two distinct values in each of the phases (e.g., 0 and 1), with a smooth change between both values in a diÔ¨Äuse region near the interface.
The problem can then be solved by integrating a set of partial diÔ¨Äerential equations for the whole system, thus avoiding the explicit treatment of the interface conditions.
First proposed in the context of solidiÔ¨Åcation and micro-structure evolution, phase Ô¨Åeld meth- ods are Ô¨Ånding ever-expanding applications (Biner, 2017), including areas such as fracture mechanics (Bourdin et al., 2000; Tann¬¥e et al., 2018; Hir- 4shikesh et al., 2019) or fatigue damage (Lo et al., 2019; Carrara et al., 2020; Kristensen and Mart¬¥ƒ±nez-PaÀúneda, 2020).
Very recently, phase Ô¨Åeld models have been developed for environmentally-assisted material degradation, in- cluding the propagation of cracks assisted by hydrogen (Mart¬¥ƒ±nez-PaÀúneda et al., 2018; Anand et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020) and the evolution of the aqueous electrolyte-metal interface due to material dissolution (StÀöahle and Hansen, 2015; Mai et al., 2016; Mai and Soghrati, 2017; Toshniwal, 2019; Gao et al., 2020).
In addition, recent works have coupled the concepts of phase Ô¨Åeld evolution due to fracture and material dissolution (Nguyen et al., 2017a,b, 2018).
In this work, we develop a new phase Ô¨Åeld formulation for simulating pitting corrosion and stress corrosion cracking.
The model builds upon the FRDR mechanism and incorporates the role of mechanics in driving inter- face kinetics and local Ô¨Ålm breakage.
The predictive capabilities of the model are demonstrated by addressing a number of relevant 2D and 3D problems, including several for which analytical or experimental data exist - a remark- able agreement is observed.

Theory In this Section we formulate our coupled deformation-diÔ¨Äusion-material dissolution theory.
First, the key mechanistic assumptions are presented.
We proceed then to develop our theory using the principle of virtual work.
Subsequently, the governing equations for our theory are formulated, as dic- tated by the coupled Ô¨Åeld equations, the free-energy imbalance and a set of thermodynamically-consistent constitutive equations. 2.1.
Phase Ô¨Åeld FRDR model Our formulation aims at capturing the process of Ô¨Ålm rupture, dissolution and repassivation.
Metals and alloys exposed to conditions of passivation are protected by a nm-size impermeable Ô¨Ålm of metal oxides and hydroxides that eÔ¨Äectively isolates the material from the corrosive environment (Macdonald, 1999).
Independently of the speciÔ¨Åc cracking mechanism, Ô¨Ålm-rupture is a necessary condition for localised damage in these conditions.
Also, a poten- tial rationale for pitting corrosion and stress corrosion cracking is the rupture of the Ô¨Ålm due to mechanical straining, followed by localised dissolution of the metal (Scully, 1980; Jivkov, 2004).
Repassivation follows each local Ô¨Ålm rupture event, with the new Ô¨Ålm being deposited on the bare metal under zero-strain conditions.
Further input of mechanical work is thus needed to fracture the new Ô¨Ålm, in a cyclic process governed by the competition be- tween Ô¨Ålming and mechanical straining kinetics.

We proceed to showcase the capabilities of the model in capturing both diÔ¨Äusion-controlled and activation-controlled corrosion.

Concluding remarks We have presented a new formulation for predicting dissolution-driven pit- ting and stress corrosion cracking.
Its main ingredients are: (i) a KKS-based 50phase Ô¨Åeld model for both activation-controlled and diÔ¨Äusion-controlled cor- rosion, (ii) a mechanics-enhanced interface kinetics law grounded on mechanochem- ical theory, and (iii) a strain rate governed Ô¨Ålm rupture and repassivation process according to the FRDR mechanism.
A numerical framework is also presented, with displacements, phase Ô¨Åeld and concentration of dissolved ions as degrees of freedom.
The model is benchmarked by addressing Ô¨Åve bound- ary value problems of particular interest.
First, we model the paradigmatic pencil electrode test to verify our numerical implementation and examine the model capabilities in predicting the transition from activation-controlled to diÔ¨Äusion-controlled corrosion.
Secondly, we reproduce C-ring experiments in a Ô¨Ålm-free environment to benchmark our mechanics-enhanced corrosion laws against experimental data.
Thirdly, SCC from an ellipsoidal pit is predicted under diÔ¨Äerent conditions to quantify the inÔ¨Çuence of mechanical stresses and their interplay with the Ô¨Ålm rupture and repassivation process.
Fourthly, the interaction between multiple pits is addressed, assessing the competing in- Ô¨Çuences of the coeÔ¨Écients characterising the mechanical, Ô¨Ålm stability and interface kinetics contributions.
Finally, pit growth in a 3D solid is predicted to showcase the capabilities of the model in capturing large scale phenomena and impacting engineering practice.
The main Ô¨Åndings are: ‚Ä¢ The interface kinetics coeÔ¨Écient L governs corrosion rates.
Corro- sion kinetics rise with increasing L, going from activation-controlled to diÔ¨Äusion-controlled corrosion, until the process becomes diÔ¨Äusion- limited, when corrosion rates become insensitive to a further augment- ing L. ‚Ä¢ Mechanical stresses and strains enhance corrosion kinetics, in agree- 51ment with experiments, favouring localised damage and triggering a pit-to-crack transition.
However, if the mechanical enhancement of L is suÔ¨Éciently large, the diÔ¨Äusion-controlled limit is reached and the inÔ¨Çuence of the mechanical contribution reaches a saturation stage.
Thus, the inÔ¨Çuence of mechanical Ô¨Åelds is most notable for activation- controlled corrosion.
‚Ä¢ The presence of a passive Ô¨Ålm exacerbates localisation and the role of mechanics.
The eÔ¨Äect is more pronounced if the Ô¨Ålm stability coeÔ¨É- cient is large as Ô¨Ålm rupture will only occur in regions where mechan- ical straining is high.
Also, cracks are more likely to nucleate under activation-controlled corrosion conditions.
Future developments could involve the extension of the model to consider other cracking mechanisms, including those driven by cathodic reactions.
Also, material microstructure and microchemistry can play an important role and should be taken into consideration in many material systems for an accurate prediction of the initial development stages.
