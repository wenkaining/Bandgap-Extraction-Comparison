Abstract
Contemporary powder-based polycrystalline nickel-base superalloys inherit microstructures and properties that are heavily deter-
mined by the thermo-mechanical treatments during processing. Here, the inÔ¨Çuence of a thermal exposure alone to an alloy powder
is studied to elucidate the controlling formation mechanisms of the strengthening precipitates using a combination of atom probe
tomography and in-situ neutron diÔ¨Äraction. The initial powder comprised a single-phase supersaturated Œ≥ only; from this, the evo-
lution of Œ≥‚Ä≤ volume fraction and lattice misÔ¨Åt was assessed. The initial powder notably possessed elemental segregation of Cr and
Co and elemental repulsion between Ni, Al and Ti with Cr; here proposed to be a precursor for subsequent Œ≥ to Œ≥‚Ä≤ phase transforma-
tions. Subsolvus heat treatments yielded a unimodal Œ≥‚Ä≤ distribution, formed during heating, with evidence supporting its formation
to be via spinodal decomposition. A supersolvus heat treatment led to the formation of this same Œ≥‚Ä≤ population during heating, but
dissolves as the temperature increases further. The Œ≥‚Ä≤ then reprecipitates as a multimodal population during cooling, here forming
by classical nucleation and growth. Atom probe characterisation provided intriguing precipitate characteristics, including clear
diÔ¨Äerences in chemistry and microstructure, depending on whether the Œ≥‚Ä≤ formed during heating or cooling.

1. Introduction
Understanding formation reactions and evolution behaviour
of ordered second phase precipitates is technologically critical
for a compendium of applications; these are most pertinent for
multi-component alloys used for structural applications. This
is acutely important for nickel-base superalloys, the material of
choice for the hottest sections of a gas turbine engines. These
alloys are designed with judicious elemental selection to confer
a balance between high temperature mechanical performance,
thermodynamic stability and corrosion resistance required for
long-term use in an aggressive in-service environment [1, 2].
The chosen multi-component compositions lead to complex mi-
crostructures and phase transformation behaviour; understand-
ing the governing mechanisms is most critical for the selection
of optimal processing parameters [3].
Modern nickel-base superalloy turbine discs are fabricated
from powders, a cost-eÔ¨Äective processing route developed to
give a step-change improvement in component performance through

This has included binary Ni-Al [9, 10], ternary Ni-Al-Cr [11],
Ni-Al-Ti [12] and multicomponent systems [13, 14, 15, 16, 17].
Model systems have low supersaturation, making it possible
to suppress Œ≥‚Ä≤ formation, then observe Œ≥‚Ä≤ forming via isother-

In order to investigate whether the formation of Œ≥‚Ä≤ occurs
by the classical nucleation mechanism or spinodal decomposi-
tion, we have studied the microstructural evolution of Œ≥‚Ä≤ from a
highly supersaturated Œ≥ phase in a nickel-base superalloy pow-

solidify exceptionally quickly, where they are subjected to cool-

simply by virtue of the heat transfer limitations from the signif-

production is hypothesised to be suÔ¨Éciently rapid to entirely
suppress the Œ≥‚Ä≤ formation, even in a highly alloyed superalloy
derivative with high concentrations of Œ≥‚Ä≤ forming elements.
In this study we have exposed a nickel-base superalloy pow-

the Œ≥‚Ä≤ solvus temperature whilst acquiring neutron diÔ¨Äraction
data in-situ. Our results reveal that Œ≥‚Ä≤ not only forms during

Furthermore, depending on the heat treatment temperature, the
distribution and morphology of Œ≥‚Ä≤ precipitates alters from an
interconnected network of precipitates, to a unimodal distribu-
tion and Ô¨Ånally to a multimodal precipitate distribution. Un-
derstanding the precipitate formation sequence is supported by
atom probe tomography measurements which ascertain charac-

subsolvus or supersolvus heat treatment.

2.4. TEM characterisation
Powders were mounted in conductive Bakelite and polished
using conventional metallographic preparation procedures with
an extended Ô¨Ånal colloidal silica stage to minimise preparation
induced deformation in the surface.
Samples for Transmis-

ples. The composition of the phases within a powder particle
were measured using a FEI Talos F200X Field Emission Gun
(Scanning) Transmission Electron Microscope (FEG-(S)TEM)
equipped with a Super X energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS)
system. Chemical distribution spectra maps were collected us-

there was no/minimal apparent overlap between the phases us-
ing Velox software.
Further TEM characterisation was conducted where diÔ¨Ärac-
tion contrast microscopy was used to produce electron diÔ¨Ärac-
tion and dark-Ô¨Åeld images. Here, a liftout procedure was used
to prepare a TEM foil using an FEI Helios G4 CX FIB operated
at 30 kV. A low, 5 kV, ion beam was used for surface cleaning
for a period of 2 minutes on each side of the TEM samples in
order to remove the outer layer of the specimens aÔ¨Äected by
FIB beam damage. The specimen was observed using a FEI
Tecnai F20 operated at 200 kV.

4.2. Precipitate formation during cooling
When Œ≥‚Ä≤ has formed during cooling, as is evident from the
sample heated to a supersolvus temperature of 1150‚ó¶C then
cooled, there is considerable evidence to indicate the precip-
itate formation mechanism is dissimilar to Œ≥‚Ä≤ formed during
heating.
The multimodal distribution is consistent with ob-
servations made by others, who attribute the generation of Œ≥‚Ä≤
from classical nucleation and growth [13, 44, 16]. Each gen-
eration of Œ≥‚Ä≤ forms during cooling, where the driving force for
Œ≥‚Ä≤ nucleation occurs from increased undercooling. Growth of
a Œ≥‚Ä≤ distribution will continue whilst the temperature is high,
though its driving force for solute diÔ¨Äusion decreases as tem-
perature becomes lower. Growth is inhibited by soft impinge-
ment when solute Ô¨Åelds overlap, however, further cooling re-
sults in non-equilibrium Œ≥‚Ä≤ compositions away from the Œ≥/Œ≥‚Ä≤
interfaces, which with further undercooling, result in a further
nucleation burst [15]. In this study, this mechanism is corrobo-
rated by (1) the presence of secondary Œ≥‚Ä≤ and tertiary Œ≥‚Ä≤ precip-
itate distributions, (2) the compositions of the precipitate dis-
tributions are dissimilar, indicating their formation at diÔ¨Äerent
temperatures as the supersaturated Œ≥ composition changes, (3)
the dissimilar morphologies of secondary Œ≥‚Ä≤ (octodendritic) and
tertiary Œ≥‚Ä≤ (spherical) indicates their growth has occurred with
precipitates from each distribution subjected to diÔ¨Äerent misÔ¨Åt
strains. The initial secondary Œ≥‚Ä≤ to form upon cooling has a lat-
tice misÔ¨Åt of -0.5%, suÔ¨Écient to favour octodendritic morphol-
ogy to develop due to the interaction between diÔ¨Äusion driven
growth and non-uniform elastic strain Ô¨Åelds around precipitates
from the Œ≥‚Ä≤ elastic anisotropy [43]. This eÔ¨Äect diminishes as
the temperature is lowered, such that the lattice misÔ¨Åt is close
to zero when the tertiary Œ≥‚Ä≤ forms, resulting in spherical precip-
itates for this distribution. Due to these factors, there was no
evidence that the Œ≥‚Ä≤ precipitates formed by spinodal decompo-
sition during cooling.
4.3. Implications
The Œ≥‚Ä≤ that has formed during heating, in the 900‚ó¶C and
1000‚ó¶C samples, is entirely a diÔ¨Äusion driven process. A clas-
sical nucleation is not plausible as there is no undercooling, and
therefore no driving force for this process. Unimodal Œ≥‚Ä≤ size
distributions, similar to the microstructure achieved here from
the sample heated to 1000‚ó¶C, have been achieved by others by
cooling samples rapidly [18, 45, 46]. There remains disagree-
ment in the literature on how such distributions form upon cool-
ing; where the precipitates are nucleation controlled, though un-
